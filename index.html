Abstractions and pattern-matching

Oversimplifying a bit, her talk was about programming abstractions from a teaching perspective. We create abstractions of bigger, more complicated concepts, and we use those abstractions to match patterns. When we teach beginners, we need to be careful about which abstractions we collectively use, because ultimately those matched patterns have consequences. Sometimes the way our abstractions were developed makes it extremely hard to figure out what purpose they originally served. For beginners, and for us as teachers.

Fat Talk

Arroyo and Harwood (2012) used the following as examples of fat talk, based on categories described by Ousley et al. (2008):

So we’re going to talk about fat talk. What is fat talk? It's a lot of things that probably sound familiar.

What one’s eating and exercise habits should be 

  • I know I shouldn’t eat this brownie.
  • I need to work out more.
  • I should watch what I eat.
I know I shouldn’t eat this brownie. I need to work out more.

Fears of becoming out of shape or overweight

  • I’m so worried that I’m going to get fat!
  • I’m scared of what I will look like if I gain weight.
  • I’d really hate to get fat.
I’m scared of what I will look like if I gain weight.

How one’s eating and exercise habits compare to others

  • Amber [Mike] works out twice as long as I do. I wish I had her [his] stamina so that I could lose weight.
  • If I ate like Jennifer [John] does, I would lose weight.
  • I wish I could eat as healthy as some of my friends do.
Mike works out twice as long as I do. I wish I had their stamina so that I could lose weight.

Other people’s shape and appearance

  • Look how much weight she [he] has gained. She [he] looks terrible!
  • I wish I was as skinny [toned] as Amber [Will].
  • How does she [he] stay so thin?
Look how much weight he has gained. He looks terrible!

One’s own weight, shape or diet

  • I’m so fat
  • My ass is huge
  • I ate way too much yesterday

Fat talk is interpersonal

"...we must know that we can be free enough, sometimes, to share the things that scare us without fear of recriminations. We must be able to talk about what is messy or sad, to have hard conversations with colleagues who are driving us crazy. We can’t be focused just on efficiency."

- What Google Learned From Its Quest to Build the Perfect Team
People use fat talk as a way to test people’s perceptions, to get reassurance, to get confirmation of suspicions, or to evaluate socially upward trajectories. Fat talk can be a means of absolution. Sometimes it can be a way to gain control by saying something bad about yourself before someone else does. It can be a way that we share what scares us.

Fat talk is mutually reinforcing

There is a social contract of engagement

Fat talk is conversational. There is practically a set script. When someone says “I wish I was thinner”, we know what’s supposed to be said next. “Me too.” Or: “Nah, you look great.” Someone says “Ugh, I need to start going to the gym. I’m so weak,” is supposed to be followed by, “Yeah, I’m totally out of shape.” Or: “Yeah, but at least you had that salad.” When other people fat talk, we feel pressured to engage. Even if we feel awesome or indifferent.

Fat talk mediates our abstractions and patterns

Arroyo and Harwood found that fat talk is a mediator. Fat talk is what exists in between weight thoughts and mental health concerns, between chronic dieting and the possibility of an eating disorder. A good day and a bad one. Fat talk, whether reinforced or shut down, negotiates the way forward for vulnerable populations. Even when you aren’t the one initiating it.

Who is assumed to be vulnerable?

The range of eating disorders, disordered eating, and those affected is a lot bigger than we tend to talk about.

When we talk about eating disorders, those vulnerable populations are often assumed to be women. Usually white women, and usually those with the means to seek treatment in the first place. Research demographics (and tech diversity measures) unfortunately rarely deviate from that expectation. The everyday assumption with eating disorders is also usually that we’re talking about anorexia or bulimia.

Binge eating can affect our workplaces, too

“beyond the already well-documented findings that binge eating is associated with distress, depression, and obesity and its associated metabolic features our findings also indicate that binge eating is associated with work productivity impairment. Employers have been slow to recognize binge eating as an important health risk behavior"

- Striegel et al. (2012)
Binge eating is associated with depression, and with obesity (if that's a thing that concerns you), but also with work productivity impairment. In a workplace culture that often waxes on about 10x and flow, employers don't seem to recognize how much this and other eating disorders can affect their workforce.

Binge eating affects all genders

“Finally, few differences were found between men and women who binge eat. Eating disorders still go largely undetected and untreated, but especially so in men."

- Striegel et al. (2012)
If you are thinking that eating disorders are low priority because tech has so few women, think again. Tech is overly full of men, but those men are also at risk for or have already have an eating disorder, and it is negligent to not talk about that.

Yes, all genders (not "both")

Eating disorder researchers often neglect the health and wellness needs of trans and non-binary people

I also have to tell you that research in this area is a big ol’ pile of gender binaries. Science is made up of scientists who are regular people, and many of the people responsible for educating us need to educate themselves.

What to do about fat talk

Can you not?

No, seriously, try a No-Fat-Talk:30

If you find yourself saying a lot of fat talk yourself, you don't have to love your body, your workout, or your food choices. You just have to pause your self-talk. Try doing it for a week. Challenge yourself to do it for a month.

Consider the implications of certain body positive phrases

  • Your Body Is Already Perfect
  • All Bodies Are Good Bodies
  • Don’t Change Your Body – Change Your Perspective
  • There’s Nothing Wrong with Your Body – There’s Something Wrong with Society

- great list by Sam Dylan Finch

Bodies are not a fixed point or a source of perfect truth. Sometimes bodies are problematic for us. Sometimes bodies have to change.

It's okay to fully disengage

You don't have to be body positive to mitigate the harmful effects of fat talk

And what if you don’t feel like you know someone well enough to respond to their fat talk that way? It may sound awkward if you’re not used to it, but you really can just leave them hanging. The next time someone complains about or justifies their food choice to you, change the subject. Ask about their dog or their kid. Ask what they’ve been working on.

You don't have to love your body to be valued.

You are never obligated to love anything, including your body.

Emphasize care and agency over perfection

  • All bodies have value. All bodies deserve care.
  • Don’t let society tell you that your body makes you less than
Some counter-examples from Sam Dylan Finch’s article: All bodies have value. All bodies deserve care. Don’t let society tell you that your body makes you less than.

Employers: don't penalize or reward food and body decisions

Be flexible. Pre-emptively encourage personal choices.

If you’re an employer or a manager, the people who work for you don’t owe you their health, but you owe them theirs. And that includes mental health. Don’t penalize or reward food choices or desk choices. If you offer free snacks, offer a variety, not just the so-called healthy ones. If you do meetups, keep doing the pizza but offer some alternatives like nuts or veggies, or make it explicitly chill to bring your own food. Maybe even try a potluck and see what happens.
